The psychogeography known at Raddle Moon

by Susan Clark

 

A shorter version of this piece was published in Sulphur 44 (Spring 1999): 189-93

 

dear Ccc&cie,

Re : The psychogeography known as RADDLE MOON

[a] book, even a fragmentary one, has a centre which attracts it. This centre is not fixed but is displaced by the pressure of the book and circumstances of its composition. Yet it is also a fixed centre which, if it is genuine, displaces itself, while remaining the same and becoming always more central, more hidden, more uncertain and more imperious. He who writes the book writes it out of desire for this centre and out of ignorance. — Blanchot

It has long been said that the desert is monotheistic. Is it illogical or devoid of interest to observe that the district in Paris between Place de la Contrescarpe and Rue de l—Arbal conduces rather to atheism, to oblivion and to the disorientation of habitual reflexes? [...] Psychogeography could set for itself the study of the precise laws and specific effects of the geographical environment, consciously organized or not, on the emotions and behaviour of individuals. — Debord, Les Lèvres Nues 6, 1955

With each new walk I carry with me the memory of the experience of all the others. —Richard Long

Can I say that out of desire for just this “uncertain,” “displaced,” “fixed” and unfixed booklike centre, and out of ignorance of that centre, Raddle moon is a kind of psychogeography which rightly resists description (incl. my own) but which draws the work which belongs to it?

I started to write this thing, as you all know, in the belief that Raddle moon had always been suspicious of pre-emptive self-description, of “platforms”; wanted (from survival instinct) to stay out the way of certain historicizing machines that prefab’d and demo’d them, wanted to believe the magazine had always declared its intentions quite simply by enacting them, etc. etc.

Wanted to make a space, not take a stand. That. (Realizing that that “that” may relate partly to our history, which I’ve tried to write for Charles.) So, Raddle moon as psychogeography—a haunted, porous “no-place,” implicated but motile, built of little but the trace of the work that’s appeared there “agglomerative, bulgy, made and unmade” what I’ve called a “utopia of shoddy upkeep,” despairing of time, money and energy—at least seems something I recognize, know.

Utopia: “In this vested, gendered, entirely fraught medium, the real question is what is being celebrated” (Laura Moriarty, in the Women/Writing/Theory colloquium in Raddle Moon 11) and “The state had come to us fragmentary. Our writing was unavoidable” (Norma Cole, ibid, ditto) for instance. An unpocketable and disheveled field, Possibility. Blanchot’s book, unfinished over and over again each issue, each work contributing to the “pull” of that more and more complexly informed, even demanding (“imperious”), centre that brings the work towards it.

If Raddle moon is that psychogeography made of the trace of writers and artists across it and by the temporary local coherences they’ve made (am I going to call that effect “community” in a moment?) it is this space that attracts our future work profoundly. And by future work, I don’t just mean work that’s out there and might think Raddle moon an appropriate venue. I mean work that hasn’t been written yet. And of course I think a lot about women’s work here. Like any editor, I know how difficult it still is to bring out women’s work. Proust said don’t be afraid to go too far because that’s where the real work is. If I’m going to be utopian, I’m going to say that’s the psychogeography I aspire to: the place where writers, especially women, feel they can go “too far.”

So, ok, it’s my job as editor to be utopian! (if for no other reason than because, as Erin so succinctly puts it, How else?) But if a “no-place” which will make future work, in some sense, possible is all I care about these days, I can’t say I’m yet clear about how to get there. Struggling with a number of, for me, unworkable notions of what it is a magazine does over the years (all that early roaming the world in search of new poetic means was necessary, perhaps, who knows) allowed me finally to recognize what courage the very existence of a periodical like Kathleen Fraser’s HOW(ever)—which I first saw years after hearing about it, and becoming aware of its effects—could give and how courage became new thought and new work.

Tish—the great Canadian example of exactly this in my own backyard—seemed more distant in time, gender and (so I then thought) poetics. So I didn’t get to see “it” happen close up until around 1989, when Barscheit (Christine Stewart, Catriona Strang, Lisa Robertson, eds.) occurred. Its very ephemerality (printed on hijacked equipment and “borrowed” paper, disseminated for free, and quickly), the sense of its constant immanence—there was no publishing schedule, so at any moment there could be a Barscheit—and its guerrilla-style protest against the silencing of unfashionable poetry/poetics (an unspoken editorial policy being to print works which would be rejected at this or that other magazine—in the same way Tish saw itself publishing what Prism couldn’t accept) made a literal space, an opening; found and amplified what Norma often calls a “conversation” and got it within earshot of others.

So: that’s a use. While I was figuring this out, of course, everything changed. Cyberspace makes a small-circulation, booklike magazine look a very strange way of finding like minds, and a certainly clumsy way of making a relational context for varied works of writing and image. (I look at Laura’s glorious online non [1] and regret the sad weight of the physical world! Can these bulky ephemerals, largely scorned after their period is up, any longer justify being printed in sweat, trees and wheelbarrowloads of cash? When they’re landfill the back issues of non will be behind a click-icon, ever-present.) Delight in the object (still evident in new beauties like The Germ, Boxkite, Rhizome, among others)—and an ever more capacious web site—is my defence, for now.

 

Dear Chas.,

Re: l’archivisme, or “the circumstance of its composition”

You kindly as ever get in the way of my cramped impulse to throw the past out. (“When I see the word ‘positioning,’ I vomit”) So, how to speak this history? In many ways, it now seems to me the record of an exploration of the use of public space. Therefore the record of my/our gradually shifting understandings of that space. Like a city of invisible but potent—or nicely temporary, but potent—architecture.

So, what happened here? Well, Raddle moon published its first issue as The raddle moon Feb.14.84 as a 6”x 9” perfectbound booklike object of about 100 pages ostensibly celebrating the tenth anniversary of the University of Victoria’s Creative Writing Department. Born—in our minds—out of sheer desire to make “something where there was nothing” (as Melissa says) in a fairly primitive, if urgent, sense.

Truth is, publishing up a wobbly literary from within a university writing department need not be a carefully deliberated act. The magazine answered a particular moment of available tools: departmental money; a computer; a background in book editing and publishing; friendship (most importantly with Kathryn MacLeod, with whom I edited the first four issues); stubbornness, energy and a sense of “leaving home” aesthetically (predictably, our relationship with the university didn’t last beyond the first issue).

Behind that energy was the compulsion Kathryn and I felt to force open a window under threat of suffocation in the stale party atmosphere of the done-to-death Canadian Content drive which only a decade earlier had come as a genuinely liberating force. As our manifesto in the third issue put it: “Perhaps because we come from an island ourselves and recognize the symptoms, we begin to see Canada as a tiny isolate land. And feel, nationally, ‘bushed’. We notice that our drawers are stuffed with airmail stationery.” Or, you might say perceived limitation (“perhaps we are just greedy: the plateful in front of us not big enough for our eyes” the same editorial continues) manifested an extreme desire which in turn catalysed adaptive evolutionary changes (as some say species’ desire does).

We were at the end of those glory days of the ’60s and ’70s when the small press publishers in Canada—Talonbooks, Anansi, Coach House, etc.—had birthed and unearthed a new national literature and urged us all in large-character posters to READ CANADIAN. We were kids, but a slightly older generation of practicing poets had found out mid-career what it was like to read, write and publish Here, not There (not Britain, not the USA). Life was fine.

For those of us still in highschool—to chart the absolute history of Raddle moon, but also of the country—it had meant that by 1969, our 24-year-old English teacher knew it was important to read Phyllis Webb’s recent Naked Poems to her class of fifteen-year-olds....Here, suddenly (3:30pm, Wednesday, in the bleak cold “New Wing”) for some unknown reason, were these miracles of deft, ironic sureness, this Raven-like trickster, shimmering with an unblinking, self-conscious intelligence, opened to paradox, the political, the sexual and language itself. Here, for some unknown reason, was this woman, this Canadian, thinking aloud in a way we’d never known a human to think or speak.

But CanLit turned out to contain no other Phyllis Webbs. It was a small literature, after all, no matter how urgently and famously it was boiling up all around. Ten years later, predictably, we didn’t get what we’d been given and you could say we were bored. Young writers we knew tended to read nothing but the same few Canadians—if they read at all (that Salinger “purity” model). Of course there was a lot else going on in Canada we didn’t know about and might not have recognized if we had. Of course, of course. It must have been very different in Vancouver—Tish being the evidence—where the Black Mountain feed had come in, but the ocean’s quite a moat, you know, and by the late seventies, at least in Victoria, it was getting a bit difficult to breathe.

So, restlessness, comradeship, and a budget morphed From An Island (which Raddle moon officially replaced) into a kind of “to-an-island”—an import rather than an export model. And for the first few issues, we had editors in places as far off-centre as possible: north of 60 (the promised multi-layered Inuit sound poetry turned out to be impossible to translate, and sacred), Haifa, Florence, Africa, Japan. And the name to name this: “moon marked with red ochre,” “earth as moon made of (red) earth”—in fact, three words we liked as a sound, a little prosody exercise.        

 

Dear Myung, [2]

Re: whatever publication

The relationship with alterity is the original case of this affliction of the present of consciousness with a past that it cannot render present, represent. The present is afflicted with a bond with something that comes to pass without being convertible into an initiative of the present, and that holds on, and in this hold distends one.  — Levinas

I’ve been reading your conversation with Yedda Morrison in Tripwire, hearing you saying things like, “For me, where I can say politicize I have to say the word generosity. Implicit in being political is trying to restore the human face.” And my attention’s caught by your mention of the problem of “unrecognizability.”

I’ve been thinking about “use” and “sufficiency” (re: Raddle moon, re: whatever publication; re: public spaces) and about evolution’s both adaptive and unforeseen solutions to life-threatening situations and how these are different from the revolutionary (revolving) solutions proposed by certain aesthetics and how much attention I’ve given them, thinking there was no other way. And thinking these things, I stumbled on Filliou’s sweet formula

! > ?

“surprise is greater than questioning” and posted it on the wall here in 72-point type because it made me just plain happy. And curious. I puzzle, laughing, “how to obey?” this cheerful directive, this good-sounding and suggestive statement. And see perhaps I’d “meant” it as the nifty condensary of your neighbouring (and here I can say maybe anti-utopian) “generosity as method.”

I might have claimed your “unrecognizability” was the poetic we went in search of writ large across syntax, form, even content. But I think what you’re talking about is somehow the opposite. Not the welcome pleasure of defamiliarization but the thing so unfamiliar you have no way of seeing it, let alone valuing it. And/but which implicates the public and implies responsibility.

My only way to think this extreme-seeming difficulty is to posit evolution again and suggest that unrecognizability is never total and that it is desire that creates adaptions—leaving us only impatience to deal with! It’s that implicated, fundamental. Always happening, in whatever arena. Can a poetics which puts its attention on Levinas— “distension,” the “bond” with the “incontrovertible,” recognize (without “converting”) the unrecognizable, I wonder? And is the genuinely public space necessarily “generous” in this manner? I mean without even trying to be? How amend editorial practice to ensure unrecognizability its opportunity? Is it impossible? Or just scary? ... And so every little organism gets to find out how inertia and fear operate on the planet.

I know that in the days when this “unrecognizability” was a gender issue, Louky Bersianik’s L’Euguelionne suggested the folly of confrontational engagement with the oppositional structures we wanted to leave behind (her formula might have been “.!>?” or, “the surprising and emphatic restatement of self/gender is greater than questioning the status quo on its own terms”). Enactment of self “as if”—however one could conceive that “as if”— and especially the reclaiming of pleasure constituted her persuasive formula for change.

So, I am trying to “be unfamiliar with what I am doing” (Cage) and it’s, of course, hard! Can welcome make visible something unforeseen (your “unrecognisable”?) I wonder. Say We is walking to a party without a gift and suddenly realizes that the street is full of gifts. He’s still We. And the “gift” he picks up is dependent on it. And this is ground we’ve been over before....

No answers; but I do want to acknowledge Roy Miki’s work for unrecognizability in Canada in West Coast Line and to point to Chain’s wonderful systemic refusal of certain forms of editorial choice coupled with their investigation of somewhat parallel writing strategies—collaborations, programmatic writing.
